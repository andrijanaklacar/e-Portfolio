Research Methods and Professional Practice

This module provides foundation on research methods and introduces research techniques, which will then be applied on upcoming projects. It will also provide insight on professional, legal, ethical, and cultural issues affecting computing professionals and projects nowadays.


Unit 1:
Collaborative Learning Discussion 1: Codes of Ethics and Professional Conduct
Reflective Activity 1 
Introduction

This reflective activity will be based on the assumption that the computing
professional in question is an employee of a private clinic and is responsible
for setting up an online appointment booking and scheduling with an additional
feature of online payments using the clinic’s system. This clinic is assumed to
be based in the United Kingdom (UK) and personal information such as: full names,
email addresses, phone numbers, patients’ addresses, ID numbers, and credit card
details will be stored in the clinic’s databases.

Ethical Issues Identified

Stahl’s et al. (2016) paper has touched upon several ethical issues regarding the
information technology sector. The following four issues have been chosen to be the
most relevant, accurate, and urgent issues based on the assumed organisation 
mentioned earlier:

  •  Consent and privacy issues; The system is expected to collect personal and
sensitive data of the clinic’s patients who wish to proceed with the online booking
method. Consent and privacy issues should be prioritised.
    • 

  • Age-related issues; Elders are less likely to use and adapt to the new and complex
technology, as well as the Internet (Guner & Acarturk, 2020).
    • 

  • Harm, misuse, deception issues; This issue was considered important, as there 
is always a chance of malicious activity by insiders (i.e., clinic’s staff) 
(Al-Harrasi et al., 2021). Clinic members and staff should have adequate and frequent 
training of the importance of ethics and the importance of data breaches.
    • 

  • Inclusion issues; This type of issue was included due to a percentage of people not
having the adequate resources (smart devices, weak or no internet connections, etcetera)
to use the newly adopted system by the clinic for appointment scheduling.
    •

Legal Issues

In the UK there are some Acts, regulations, and standards which refer to data, data breaches,
and payments and all organisations in the country should be compliant with these. For example, 
the Data Protection Act 1998 (Legislation, 2022a) refers to the way an individual’s personal 
information may be used by organisations, businesses, and government, as well as one’s right 
to ask for their personal information. Following up is the Computer Misuse Act 1990 
(Legislation, 2022b). This Act refers to the security of any computer material and factors that
could breach this security are unauthorised access and/or modification of one’s data. Lastly,
since the system can ask for credit card details, the Payment Card Industry Data Security 
Standard (PCI DSS) (PCI Security Standards Council, 2021) should be reviewed. This standard 
provides an insight on security standards and its goal is to secure any debit or credit card
transactions from data theft and/or fraud.

Ethical/Social and Technical Issues

As there are indeed several ethical issues that need to be addressed, a computing professional
is obliged to address them. To begin with, the clinic’s system is bound to store sensitive and
personal data of patients and staff and is therefore it is required that patients and staff 
should be asked for data consent and privacy (Shaw & Erren, 2021; Price & Cohen, 2019). Other 
issues that were identified were the age and inclusion issues. These can be prevented, minimised,
and avoided by ensuring the clinic does not fully substitute the online booking system with the
traditional way. Another way of minimising the issue is to make the system more accessible and
beginner-friendly for those who struggle with technology, as they will be more intrigued and 
accept the system easier (Chang et al., 2019), with a brief yet detailed training on how to use 
the system. As mentioned earlier, not all are familiar with technology, and not every patient is
required to own adequate resources to book an appointment with the clinic.

Last but not least, adequate training in ethics, privacy, and cyberattacks/cybercrimes should be 
done at least once per year. With the rates of cybercrime and cyberattacks rapidly increasing 
worldwide, it is of great importance that this factor should be addressed thoroughly. Training and
education should not only be included for the usage of the platform, but it should also be included
in one’s induction period to ensure that malicious insiders are warned and/or discouraged. Everyone
involved with the clinic, including patients, should be informed about cyberattacks such as phishing,
data breaches, etcetera and the clinic should have constantly updated cybersecurity policies and best
practices (Rahman et al., 2020; Aldawood & Skinner, 2019; Li, et al., 2019).

Conclusion

Adopting new technology in an organisation is never easy, as there will always be ethical, legal, and 
technical issues that should be considered, reviewed, and addressed. In this activity, a clinic’s 
appointment scheduling system identified a few ethical issues, as well as legal and technical ones.
Data consent is a must when collecting data, while technology is not familiar to everyone; therefore, 
it cannot solely replace the human factor in organisations, government, and businesses. Training and 
education are two vital factors when it comes to any digitalisation process, as cybersecurity is 
important to ensure ethics and data security.
